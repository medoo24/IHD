MI Clinical Reference

Definition & Classification

Myocardial infarction (MI) is defined as irreversible cardiomyocyte death resulting from prolonged ischemia. While classically associated with plaque rupture, it is classified into five distinct types based on the underlying pathophysiological mechanism.

Type 1: Spontaneous MI

Acute atherothrombotic infarction caused by the disruption (rupture or erosion) of an atherosclerotic plaque, leading to superimposed coronary thrombosis.

Type 2: Ischemic Imbalance

Infarction resulting from an oxygen supply-demand imbalance in the absence of acute atherothrombosis. Causes include severe anemia, hypoxemia, hypotension, sustained tachyarrhythmia, coronary vasospasm, or severe hypertension.

Type 3: Cardiac Death

Cardiac death accompanied by symptoms or electrocardiographic evidence highly suggestive of acute ischemia, occurring before cardiac biomarkers (troponin) can be measured or begin to rise.

Type 4: PCI Related

Infarction associated with percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI), which includes procedure-related infarction (e.g., side-branch occlusion) or acute stent thrombosis.

Type 5: CABG Related

Infarction associated with coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) surgery.

Clinical Presentation

Classic Presentation

Acute-onset, prolonged (>10–30 minutes) retrosternal pressure, heaviness, or tightness at rest.

  • Radiation to left arm, shoulders, neck, jaw, back, or epigastrium.
  • Diaphoresis (most common associated symptom)
  • Breathlessness & Nausea/Vomiting
  • Anxiety (feeling of impending doom) & Syncope

Atypical or "Silent" Presentations

Atypical presentations are incredibly common and dangerous. Patients may present solely with isolated dyspnea, unusual weakness, acute confusion/delirium, or isolated epigastric discomfort.

👵

Older Adults

May present primarily with decompensation of another organ system or altered mental status.

🩸

Diabetes Mellitus

Progressive autonomic neuropathy can blunt ischemic pain perception.

👩

Women

Frequently report nausea, fatigue, or epigastric pain as their primary symptoms.

🫀

Heart Transplant

Atypical due to surgical denervation of the heart.

Evolution of STEMI Changes

Hyperacute (tall/peaked) T waves ➔ ST-segment elevation ➔ Pathologic Q waves ➔ T-wave inversion ➔ ST normalization.

Biomarker Kinetics

Cardiac Troponin (I & T)

Most sensitive & specific marker

Begins to rise: 4 hours
Peaks: 24 - 48 hours
Remains elevated: 7 - 10 days
Excellent for late diagnosis, but poor for detecting early reinfarction due to prolonged elevation.

CK-MB

Creatine Kinase-MB

Begins to rise: 6 - 12 hours
Peaks: 16 - 24 hours
Returns to normal: Within 48 hours
Preferred marker for diagnosing early reinfarction occurring shortly after the initial event.

Histological Evolution

0 – 24 hours

Early Coagulative Necrosis

Begins between 4 and 24 hours. Characterized by wavy fibers, cell content release, edema, and hemorrhage.

Reperfusion injury: Calcium influx and free radicals provoke hypercontraction of myofibrils, visible as dark, eosinophilic "contraction bands".

1 – 3 days

Extensive Coagulative Necrosis & Inflammation

The tissue surrounding the infarct becomes acutely inflamed, marked by a dense infiltration of neutrophils.

3 – 14 days

Macrophage Infiltration & Granulation

Macrophages infiltrate the area to degrade and clear necrotic debris. This is followed by the formation of highly vascularized granulation tissue at the margins.

Weeks to months

Scar Formation

The granulation tissue matures into a contracted, dense, gray-white collagenous scar.

Acute Stabilization & Reperfusion

Immediate stabilization aims to halt thrombosis and reduce myocardial oxygen demand.

Medical Therapy

  • Antiplatelets & Anticoagulants Aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor (prasugrel/ticagrelor preferred if PCI planned). Parenteral anticoagulation (heparin/LMWH).
  • Anti-ischemics (Nitrates) Relieve angina, reduce preload.
    CONTRAINDICATED in RV (inferior) infarct, hypotension, or recent PDE-5 inhibitor use.
  • Beta-blockers Reduce mortality and demand (if no shock or acute HF).

Reperfusion Strategy (STEMI)

Preferred: Primary PCI

Absolute preferred therapy. Goal is Door-to-Balloon time of <90 mins.

Alternative: Fibrinolysis

If PCI cannot be done within 120 mins. Indicated if symptom onset <12 hrs and no absolute contraindications.

Complications Timeline

Post-MI complications are directly tied to the histological state of the myocardium.

First Day
0 - 24 hours

Electrical Instability & Pump Failure

VF and VT are the most common complications and leading cause of sudden cardiac death pre-hospital. Cardiogenic shock and acute heart failure peak here.

1 - 3 Days

Peri-infarction Fibrinous Pericarditis

Driven by neutrophil infiltration. Presents with a friction rub and pleuritic chest pain. Generally self-limited.

3 - 14 Days
Maximum Vulnerability

Macrophages degrade necrotic tissue before a firm scar forms, causing mechanical weakness.

Papillary Muscle Rupture

Days 2-7. Usually posteromedial (PDA supply). Causes severe MR, flash pulmonary edema, shock.

Ventricular Septal Rupture

Days 3-5. Harsh holosystolic murmur, L-to-R shunt, step-up in O2 sat in RV.

LV Free-Wall Rupture

5 days - 2 weeks. Catastrophic hemopericardium, tamponade, PEA, rapid death.

Weeks - Months

Dressler Syndrome (2-10 weeks)

Autoimmune fibrinous pericarditis against released myocardial antigens.

True Ventricular Aneurysm

Thinned fibrotic scar bulges outward. Risk of mural thrombus, persistent ST elevation, late arrhythmias.
