Antithrombotic Therapy

Guidelines for preventing atherothrombotic events.

Single Antiplatelet for Stable Disease

For patients with established chronic coronary disease who do not have a recent acute event or intervention, long-term single antiplatelet therapy (most commonly aspirin) is the standard of care to prevent atherothrombotic events, provided the patient does not have a high bleeding risk.

Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT) Duration & Selection

DAPT (aspirin + P2Y12 receptor inhibitor) is indicated after an acute coronary syndrome (ACS) or percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) to prevent stent thrombosis and spontaneous recurrent atherothrombotic events.

Drug Selection: Ticagrelor and prasugrel offer more potent and predictable platelet inhibition and are generally preferred over clopidogrel for suitable ACS patients undergoing PCI.

Lipid Management

Secondary prevention through cholesterol control.

High-Intensity Statins

High-intensity or maximally tolerated statin therapy is a foundational component of secondary prevention. Statins inhibit hepatic cholesterol synthesis, lowering LDL cholesterol, decreasing vascular inflammation, improving endothelial function, and promoting plaque stabilization to prevent rupture.

Thresholds for Non-Statin Additions

Following an ACS, a fasting lipid panel should be repeated 4 to 8 weeks after therapy initiation/adjustment. If LDL cholesterol remains at or above 70 mg/dL despite maximally tolerated statin therapy, guidelines recommend adding non-statin therapies.

Option 1: Ezetimibe Decreases intestinal cholesterol absorption.
Option 2: PCSK9 Inhibitors Preserve hepatic LDL receptors for profound LDL reduction.

Neurohormonal Blockade & LV Protection

Protecting the myocardium and preventing remodeling.

ACE Inhibitors / ARBs

Inhibit the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system, reducing vasoconstriction, sodium retention, and maladaptive ventricular remodeling.

Strong Indications:

  • Reduced LV ejection fraction
  • Anterior myocardial infarction
  • Hypertension
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Chronic kidney disease

Beta-Blockers

Reduce myocardial oxygen demand by lowering heart rate, contractility, and blood pressure. Highly effective antianginal agents.

Routine long-term therapy is NOT automatically mandated for every patient with chronic coronary disease.

Firmly Established For:

  • Recent myocardial infarction
  • Reduced LV ejection fraction
  • Concurrent hypertension
  • Arrhythmias

Device Therapy Timing

Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator (ICD) criteria and timing.

An ICD continuously monitors ventricular rhythm to detect/terminate life-threatening arrhythmias, preventing sudden cardiac death.

Primary Prevention Deferral

Decisions must be deferred until after recovery and optimization of medical therapy. Reason: Stunned myocardium may spontaneously regain function early after MI or revascularization.

Secondary Prevention

Indicated for survivors of cardiac arrest or sustained hemodynamically important ventricular tachycardia (if not caused solely by a reversible trigger).

Lifestyle Modifications

Crucial interventions beyond pharmacotherapy.

Smoking Cessation

Halting tobacco exposure is critical. Smoking promotes endothelial dysfunction, oxidative stress, inflammation, vasoconstriction, thrombosis, and reduces blood oxygen capacity.

Recommended: Behavioral support and evidence-based pharmacotherapy.
Not Preferred First-Line: Electronic cigarettes (due to uncertain long-term cardiovascular and pulmonary safety).

Cardiac Rehabilitation

Structured cardiac rehabilitation programs are strongly recommended after an ACS or revascularization whenever feasible.

Program Components:

Supervised Exercise Risk-Factor Management Nutritional Intervention Medication Support Psychological Care Return-to-work Planning

Benefits: Improves functional capacity, endothelial function, autonomic balance, quality of life, and reduces recurrent cardiovascular morbidity.
