Pathophysiological Basis

Building on the principles of oxygen supply and demand and plaque biology, Chronic Coronary Syndromes (CCS) occur when these mechanisms produce a stable, predictable pattern of ischemia.

Plaque Stability vs. Vulnerability

Unlike ACS (driven by vulnerable, ruptured plaques), CCS is driven by a stable, fibrotic atherosclerotic plaque. This fixed narrowing allows adequate rest flow but critically limits coronary flow reserve during exertion.

Oxygen Supply & Demand

Symptoms appear when myocardial oxygen demand (heart rate, contractility, wall stress) exceeds maximal flow across the fixed stenosis. Ischemia is transient, subendocardial, and resolves with rest.

Coronary Steal Physiology

Distal to a stenosis, vessels are maximally dilated at rest. Potent vasodilators (adenosine) dilate healthy vessels, creating a path of least resistance that "steals" blood away from diseased beds, provoking ischemia.

Diagnostic Testing

Tests expose flow reserve limitations by increasing myocardial demand or via pharmacological vasodilation.

Resting ECG & Biomarkers

  • May reveal prior infarction, baseline ST-T abnormalities, or conduction disease.
  • Frequently completely normal between ischemic attacks.
  • High-sensitivity troponin is usually normal (not a screening tool, used to rule out ACS).

Stress Testing & Imaging

  • Exercise ECG: Looks for horizontal/downsloping ST depression. Lower accuracy if baseline ECG is abnormal.
  • Stress Echo: Looks for new/worsening regional wall-motion abnormalities.
  • Nuclear Perfusion: Identifies reversible (ischemia) vs fixed (scar) defects.
Vasodilator stress agents (dipyridamole, adenosine) are contraindicated in active asthma, severe bradycardia/heart block, and hypotension.

Coronary CT Angiography (CCTA)

Provides high-resolution, non-invasive anatomical visualization of the coronary lumen and plaque burden. Highly effective for excluding obstructive CAD in appropriately selected patients.

Invasive Coronary Angiography

The reference standard for defining coronary anatomy. Reserved for severe/refractory symptoms, high-risk non-invasive test results, or planned revascularization.

Fractional Flow Reserve (FFR): Measured during angiography to determine the true physiological significance of intermediate lesions.

Management Strategy

Management combines immediate symptom relief with long-term prevention of atherothrombotic events.

{/* Event Prevention */}

Event Prevention (Disease-Modifying)

Aims to halt plaque progression and prevent thrombosis.

  • Lifestyle: Smoking cessation, weight management.
  • Statins: High-intensity to lower LDL cholesterol.
  • Antiplatelet: Single-therapy (Aspirin typically) unless dual indication exists.
  • ACEi/ARBs: Recommended, especially with HTN, DM, CKD, or LV dysfunction.
{/* Symptom Relief */}

Symptom Relief (Anti-Anginal)

  • Beta-blockers: First-line. Reduce HR & contractility (lowers demand, prolongs diastolic perfusion).
  • Calcium-Channel Blockers (CCBs): Excellent alternatives/add-ons that induce vasodilation.
  • Nitrates: Dilate capacitance veins, decreasing venous return (preload) and ventricular wall stress.
  • Ranolazine: For refractory angina. Reduces intracellular calcium overload in myocytes.
{/* Revascularization */}

Revascularization Indications

Indicated to improve symptoms when optimal medical therapy fails, or to improve survival in patients with high-risk anatomy.

PCI

Percutaneous Coronary Intervention is favored for focal, less complex disease where rapid recovery is desired.

CABG

Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting is preferred for left main disease, complex multivessel disease (especially in diabetics), or if concomitant cardiac surgery is required.

Special Syndromes

{/* Prinzmetal */}

Prinzmetal (Variant) Angina

Caused by transient vasospasm of epicardial coronary arteries, often occurring at rest or at night. Produces transient ST-segment elevation on ECG.

Patient Profile & Triggers:
  • Young smokers lacking major risk factors
  • Cocaine, alcohol, triptans
Treatment & Contraindications:
  • Treat: CCBs (prevent spasm) & nitrates.
  • Avoid: Beta-blockers (leave alpha vasoconstriction unopposed) and Aspirin (aggravates attacks).
{/* Microvascular */}

Microvascular & Non-Obstructive Angina

(INOCA / ANOCA)

Patients present with classic angina and objective evidence of ischemia, but have absolutely no flow-limiting epicardial blockages on angiogram.

Pathophysiology & Management:

Results from impaired small-vessel dilation or abnormal microvascular constriction.

Management requires careful risk-factor control and tailored antianginal therapy based on the dominant mechanism (impaired reserve vs. hyper-constriction).
